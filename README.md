# Infinity, Lemniscate Knots, and Arithmetic Topology
It's no coincidence that the symbol for infinity is a kind of know-like object, a figure-eight turned on its side. There is a similar kind of mathematical knot called the Lemiscate knot. This page explains a conceptual analogy between the infinity symbol, mathematical knots, and number theory, ultimately suggesting that infinity can be viewed as a kind of “prime” that Euclid forgot.

---

## 1. The infinity symbol as a lemniscate

The usual mathematical symbol for infinity, \( \infty \), is a planar **lemniscate**: a closed curve in the plane that looks like a figure‑eight on its side. Algebraically, the classic lemniscate can be written as
\[
(x^{2} + y^{2})^{2} = x^{2} - y^{2},
\]
which is a self‑intersecting algebraic curve with a single crossing at the origin. [web:25][web:27]

In this sense, the infinity symbol \( \infty \) is **not a knot** in the pure topological sense, because:
- It is a **planar curve**.
- It **self‑intersects** at the center.

Topologically, knots are non‑self‑intersecting embeddings of a circle \( S^{1} \) into \( \mathbb{R}^{3} \).

---

## 2. Lemniscate knots: a 3D counterpart to ∞

Even though the infinity symbol itself is not a knot, there is a family of knots called **lemniscate knots** that are geometrically very similar. [web:17][web:19]

### 2.1. What lemniscate knots are

Lemniscate knots are constructed as:
- Closures of braids whose strands all follow the same **generalized lemniscate** (a figure‑eight or Lissajous‑type loop) in the horizontal plane.
- These braids are then embedded into \( \mathbb{R}^{3} \) so that the strands **do not self‑intersect**.

The result is a smooth, **non‑self‑intersecting knot** in 3D whose projection onto a plane can look very much like the infinity symbol \( \infty \), but without the planar crossing. [web:17][web:19][web:23]

### 2.2. The analogy

- The **2D lemniscate** \( \infty \) is like the *shadow* of a knot.
- The **lemniscate knot** is the *actual knotted object* in 3D that casts that shadow.

So, in analogy:
> **The infinity symbol \( \infty \) ≈ a planar lemniscate ≈ the projection of a lemniscate knot in 3D.**

## 3. Primes as knots in arithmetic topology

In **arithmetic topology**, number theory and 3‑manifold theory are formally compared:

| Number‑theoretic notion                 | Topological analogy                    |
|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Integers \( \mathbb{Z} \)              | 3‑sphere \( S^{3} \)                  |
| Prime ideal \( (p) \subset \mathbb{Z} \) | Knot \( K \subset S^{3} \)            |
| Ring of integers in a number field \( \mathcal{O}_K \) | Cover of \( S^{3} \) |
| Galois group \( \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \) | Fundamental / mapping‑class group of \( S^{3} \setminus K \) |

Under this dictionary:
- **Prime numbers** correspond to **knots** in a 3‑manifold.
- The primes are the “atoms” of the ring of integers, just as knots are the “minimal nontrivial” closed curves in \( S^{3} \). [web:17][web:21]

In this sense, **each prime enforces a kind of topological obstruction**, analogous to how a knot obstructs \( S^{3} \) from being “trivial.”

## 4. Infinity as the forgotten prime

Now we can make the more poetic (but conceptually coherent) move:

> **Infinity can be thought of as a kind of “prime” — the prime that Euclid forgot.**

What does this mean?

### 4.1. What Euclid worked with

Euclid’s classical number theory is discrete and finite:
- His **fundamental theorem of arithmetic** decomposes integers into finite products of primes.
- In this world, **infinity is not a number**; it is a **limit** or **boundary** concept:
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty} n, \qquad \lim_{q\to 0^+} \frac{1}{q}, \qquad \text{etc.}
\]

Euclid’s framework has no place for infinity as an algebraic object; it only appears as a *direction* or *limit*.

### 4.2. Infinity as a lemniscate‑like obstruction

In the lemniscate‑knot analogy:
- The **planar lemniscate \( \infty \)** represents a kind of **global obstruction**: the loop that never ends, yet folds back on itself.
- The **lemniscate knot** in 3D realizes this as a non‑trivial, closed, knotted curve.

If primes correspond to knots capturing **finite arithmetic obstructions**, then one can imagine **infinity** as a Knudsen‑type or “boundary” prime capturing the **infinite, global obstruction** to compactness or finiteness.

In this picture:
- **Ordinary primes** \( p \) are the “local” obstructions in \( \operatorname{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) \).
- **Infinity** is the “archimedean prime” or “prime at infinity” (used in number‑theoretic geometry) that encodes the **asymptotic behavior** of numbers on the real line.

So, informally:

> **The infinity symbol \( \infty \) is a planar lemniscate, an infinite cycle, and in the arithmetic‑topological picture it behaves like the “prime at infinity” — the prime that Euclid forgot because his universe was finite and discrete.**

---

## 5. Summary of the analogy

- **Lemniscate \( \infty \)**: a self‑intersecting planar figure‑eight curve.
- **Lemniscate knot**: a 3D knot whose projection looks like \( \infty \), but without self‑intersection.
- **Prime numbers**: in arithmetic topology, primes correspond to knots in a 3‑manifold.
- **Infinity**: in the same spirit, can be viewed as a “prime at infinity” — the lemniscate‑like obstruction that Euclid never encoded algebraically, yet is essential for understanding global arithmetic and limits.

This is not a theorem, but a **dictionary** and **philosophical analogy** connecting:
- the shape of \( \infty \),
- the topology of knots,
- and the arithmetic of primes.
